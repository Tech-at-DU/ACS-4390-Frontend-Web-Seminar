

# ACS 4390 — Final Presentation Rubric

Students will present their research topic and prototype to the class. Presentations should demonstrate a clear understanding of the topic, evidence of research, and a working prototype or experiment.

Typical presentation length: **10–15 minutes**.

---

## Evaluation Criteria

| Criteria | Excellent (3) | Satisfactory (2) | Needs Work (1) |
|--------|---------------|------------------|---------------|
| **Topic Understanding** | Demonstrates deep understanding of the technology or concept. Explains how it works and why it exists. | Basic understanding of the topic. Some concepts unclear or incomplete. | Limited understanding. Explanation is inaccurate or missing key concepts. |
| **Research Quality** | Uses multiple strong sources and clearly explains key ideas from research. | Some research evident but limited depth or few sources. | Little evidence of research or sources are weak. |
| **Prototype / Experiment** | Working prototype clearly demonstrates the concept being studied. | Prototype exists but is limited or only partially demonstrates the concept. | Prototype missing or does not demonstrate the topic. |
| **Technical Explanation** | Clearly explains architecture, trade-offs, or implementation details. | Some technical explanation but lacks depth or clarity. | Technical explanation unclear or missing. |
| **Presentation Clarity** | Presentation is well organized and easy to follow. Visuals or demos support explanation. | Organization somewhat unclear or explanation difficult to follow. | Disorganized presentation or difficult to understand. |
| **Demo or Examples** | Live demo or clear examples effectively show the technology in practice. | Demo exists but unclear or limited. | No demo or examples provided. |

---

## Scoring

Each category is scored **1–3 points**.

| Total Score | Assessment |
|-------------|-----------|
| **16–18** | Excellent work |
| **12–15** | Good understanding with some gaps |
| **8–11** | Basic effort but significant weaknesses |
| **6–7** | Incomplete presentation |

---

## Presentation Expectations

A strong presentation should include:

1. **Introduction** – What problem or technology are you studying?
2. **Background** – How the system or concept works.
3. **Research Findings** – What you learned from sources.
4. **Prototype / Experiment** – Demonstrate your project.
5. **Analysis** – Strengths, weaknesses, and trade-offs.
6. **Conclusion** – What developers should know about this topic.

---

## Common Problems to Avoid

- Slides that only repeat documentation
- No working demo or experiment
- Explaining *what* a tool is without explaining *why it exists*
- Presentations that are mostly marketing language instead of technical explanation

The goal of this presentation is not to promote a tool or framework. The goal is to **analyze and explain how a technology works and when developers should use it.**
