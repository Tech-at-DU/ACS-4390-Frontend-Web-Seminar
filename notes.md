ACS 4390 — Front End Web Tech Seminar

Course Introduction

This course is not a tutorial class.
It is not a framework walkthrough.
It is not a “learn X tool” course.

This is a seminar.

In this class, you will choose a focused topic in the frontend UI domain, research it deeply, and become the temporary expert in the room. You will present your findings, defend your reasoning, and lead a serious technical discussion.

The goal is not coverage.
The goal is depth.

⸻

Why This Course Exists

The frontend landscape is changing rapidly. AI tools can generate boilerplate code. Frameworks evolve quickly. Tutorials are everywhere.

What remains scarce — and valuable — are engineers who can:
	•	Evaluate tradeoffs
	•	Defend architectural decisions
	•	Critique tools critically
	•	Understand systems, not just syntax
	•	Connect design decisions to technical consequences
	•	Lead technical conversations

This seminar is designed to help you develop those skills.

⸻

What You Will Do

Each student will:
	1.	Select a focused topic within frontend UI.
	2.	Conduct meaningful research beyond surface-level documentation.
	3.	Produce a structured technical briefing.
	4.	Present and teach the topic to the class.
	5.	Lead a guided discussion.
	6.	Respond to critique and questioning.

Topics may fall into areas such as:
	•	UI systems and architecture
	•	Performance and browser internals
	•	Design systems and interface thinking
	•	Accessibility and usability at scale
	•	AI and frontend workflows
	•	Experimental or creative interface systems

However, topics must be analytical and defensible.
This is not a demo session.

⸻

What Is Expected of You

This is an advanced course. You are expected to:
	•	Come prepared
	•	Contribute to discussion regularly
	•	Ask hard questions
	•	Offer constructive critique
	•	Engage seriously with your peers’ work
	•	Think beyond “how” and focus on “why”

If you prefer passive lectures, this course will be uncomfortable.

That discomfort is intentional.

⸻

What This Course Is Not
	•	Not a coding bootcamp
	•	Not a design studio
	•	Not a tutorial series
	•	Not a framework showcase

You will not be graded on how flashy your slides are.
You will be graded on depth, clarity, rigor, and your ability to think critically.

⸻

What You Should Leave With

By the end of the semester, you should be able to:
	•	Articulate and defend complex frontend decisions
	•	Evaluate tools beyond hype
	•	Think in systems rather than features
	•	Lead technical discussions professionally

How to Choose a Researchable Topic

Some topics may sound abstract or large. Your job is to narrow them into something concrete and investigable.

If you lack real-world scaling experience, use one of these approaches:

1. Case Study Approach  
Choose an existing, documented system (e.g., a public design system, open-source project, or well-known application) and analyze:
   • Its structure
   • Its stated goals
   • Tradeoffs it makes
   • Known limitations or criticisms

2. Comparative Analysis  
Compare two competing approaches and evaluate:
   • Where each performs well
   • Where each breaks down
   • What constraints influence the choice

3. Small-Scale Experiment  
Build a constrained prototype to test a specific idea and measure:
   • Complexity
   • Performance
   • Maintainability
   • Developer experience

Your topic does not require industry experience.
It requires careful framing, research, and analysis.

If a topic feels too large, narrow it until:
   • You can define a specific question
   • You can identify competing approaches
   • You can explain what would count as success or failure
